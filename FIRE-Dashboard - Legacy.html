🪜 Lifetime Withdrawal Strategy — tax-bracket-optimized mix across all retirement years

Every year in retirement, three pools are available (eventually): Trad 401K (taxed at ordinary rate when withdrawn), Roth 401K (tax-free forever), and Taxable (stocks + cash, LTCG rates). The optimal year-by-year mix depends on how much other income (Social Security, pension) you have that year. This card fills the low tax brackets with Trad withdrawals (cheap tax), then tops up with Roth (tax-free) — minimizing lifetime tax. It also respects 59.5 lock and 73+ RMD rules.

📖 New to this? Click to expand — explains the strategy + tax terms (MFJ, 12% bracket, RMD, LTCG, AMT) in plain English

🎯 The Strategy in 30 Seconds

You have four "buckets" to draw from in retirement: Trad 401K (ordinary tax on withdrawal), Roth (tax-free forever — already paid), Taxable brokerage (stocks — pay LTCG on the gain portion), and Cash (no tax). Each year, the algorithm picks the mix that minimizes lifetime tax, not just this year's tax.

The 3-phase rulebook:

  1. Pre-59.5 (the "Bridge") — 401K is locked by the IRS (10% early-withdrawal penalty). Pull from Taxable stocks and Cash. With no W-2 income, the gain portion often lands in the 0% LTCG bracket — effectively tax-free harvesting.
  2. Age 59.5–72 (the "Flex Zone") — 401K unlocks. Pull just enough Trad to fill the cheap 12% bracket (~$124K/yr MFJ gross = std ded + 12% cap). Top up with Roth (tax-free) instead of pushing into the 22% bracket.
  3. Age 73+ (the "Forced Draw") — IRS requires minimum distributions (RMDs) from Trad regardless of need. Take those first, then top up with whatever's cheapest that year.

📖 Tax Terms — Plain English Glossary

MFJ Married Filing Jointly — US tax status where spouses file one combined return. Brackets are ~2× single filer, so couples get more "cheap room" at each rate. (You + Rebecca = MFJ.) Standard Deduction A flat amount subtracted from your gross income before tax is calculated. 2026 MFJ ≈ $30K, Single ≈ $15K. If your income is under the std ded → $0 federal tax. Top of 12% Bracket The taxable-income ceiling where your marginal rate jumps from 12% to 22%. 2026 MFJ: $94,300. Combined with the std ded, you can pull ~$124K of Trad 401K per year and pay only ~8% effective tax. This is the "sweet spot" the strategy targets. Top of 22% Bracket Next tier up — 22% → 24%. 2026 MFJ: $201,050. Above here, each extra dollar gets expensive fast (24% → 32% → 35% → 37%). LTCG Long-Term Capital Gains — tax on profit from selling investments held >1 year. Three brackets: 0% up to ~$94K MFJ total taxable income, 15% up to ~$584K, 20% above. Gains stack on TOP of your ordinary income for this calculation. RMD Required Minimum Distribution — starting at age 73, the IRS forces a minimum annual withdrawal from Trad 401K/IRA, based on a life-expectancy divisor. Taxed as ordinary income whether you need the cash or not. Miss it = 25% penalty. AMT Alternative Minimum Tax — a parallel US tax system that catches high earners using lots of deductions. Rarely hits retirees. Different story in Taiwan: Taiwan's Income Basic Tax (IBT) is a flat 20% on worldwide income above ~NT$7.5M (~$250K USD) — far above a $60K retirement spend. Roth vs Trad Roth = tax paid on the way in, so withdrawals are tax-free forever and have no RMD. Trad = tax deferred on the way in, so withdrawals are taxed as ordinary income. Both unlock at 59.5; both get 10% penalty if pulled earlier. NIIT Net Investment Income Tax — extra 3.8% on investment income above $250K MAGI (MFJ). Not relevant at typical retirement spend levels. SS Social Security — up to 85% of benefits are taxable as ordinary income (depending on provisional income). The chart models this conservatively at 85%.

💡 Why This Order?

Cheap brackets are a use-it-or-lose-it offer. Every year, the IRS hands you a fresh std ded + fresh 12% bracket of "cheap room." Unused room expires at year-end. The algorithm uses that room every single year to drain Trad at 8–12%, rather than letting Trad balloon and hit 22–32% later when RMDs force big draws.

Roth is the emergency reserve — tax-free, no RMD, flexible. It's saved for years when pulling more Trad would shove you into expensive brackets (e.g., a big one-time expense, or post-72 when SS + RMD already eat your cheap room). Pulling Roth prevents the rate from climbing that year.
